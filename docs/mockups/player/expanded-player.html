About

Nobel laureate Daniel Kahneman explores how systematic biases in human judgment lead to predictable errors. Drawing from decades of research with Amos Tversky, he examines the interplay between fast, intuitive "System 1" thinking and slower, deliberate "System 2" reasoning — and what that means for every decision we make.

The concept of cognitive bias was first systematically described by Kahneman and Tversky in 1972. When people rely on heuristic mental shortcuts, they expose themselves to systematic errors in judgment that can be both predicted and measured. This insight fundamentally changed how economists and psychologists model human behavior. The implications for policy, medicine, and business have proven far-reaching.
